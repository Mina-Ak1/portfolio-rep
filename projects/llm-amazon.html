How do LLMs and classical methods classify Amazon reviews?

Python Transformers TF-IDF Logistic Regression DistilBERT t-SNE PCA Scikit-learn HuggingFace

TL;DR. I built a balanced 6-class dataset (5,000 reviews per category) and compared TF-IDF baselines (Logistic Regression, Random Forest) with a fine-tuned DistilBERT. DistilBERT performs best overall, but the Office Products vs Electronics boundary remains tricky. Short, generic reviews are the hardest for all models.

Categories: Beauty & Personal Care, Books, Electronics, Grocery & Gourmet Food, Toys & Games, Office Products.

Data source: Amazon Reviews 2023 (McAuley Lab)

ModelTrain AccuracyTest Accuracy
Logistic Regression (TF-IDF)0.870.84
Random Forest (TF-IDF)0.860.80
DistilBERT (fine-tuned)0.950.88

Among all categories, Electronics and Office Products have the highest number of false positive cases, while Grocery and Gourmet Food shows the lowest false positives, especially in the fine-tuned DistilBERT model. This contrast makes them a good set for comparison when visualizing how mixed the categories appear after PCA transformation.

What did I actually build?

A comparison: TF-IDF with Logistic Regression & Random Forest vs. a fine-tuned distilbert-base-uncased. Balanced sampling keeps per-class comparisons fair and the evaluation simple.

Which approach works better overall?

DistilBERT comes out ahead.

Where do models struggle?

Office Products ↔ Electronics is the toughest boundary across all approaches.

Do shorter reviews hurt accuracy?

Yes. Short, generic reviews (like “Great product, works fine”) lack anchors, so models guess more. Below is the review length vs. misclassification view.

What does the representation space look like?

To see the difference between bag-of-words vs contextual embeddings, I plotted t-SNE for TF-IDF and for DistilBERT embeddings. TF-IDF shows heavy mixing, especially Office/Electronics. DistilBERT reveals tighter groups; Grocery is notably well-separated.

What does the LLM focus on?

A quick look at DistilBERT’s [CLS] attention maps: correct cases highlight product-specific terms; misclassified pairs lean on generic sentiment or accessory words that appear in multiple categories.
